## PART 2: DESIGN PATTERNS — TOP 5 PATTERNS ALL SUCCESSFUL BLINDS SITES USE

**Pattern 1: Full-Bleed Lifestyle Hero Imagery**
Every successful competitor leads with large, aspirational photography of real London/UK homes with their products installed. The image does the selling — not the copy. Hillarys, Shuttercraft, and Shutterly Fabulous all use this pattern. Adams Blinds currently uses no hero image.

**Pattern 2: Persistent Trust Bar Above Navigation**
A horizontal strip above the main nav carrying 3-4 trust icons (guarantee, rating, years in business, finance) appears on Hillarys and Thomas Sanderson. This instantly communicates legitimacy before the visitor reads anything. It's the first thing eyes land on after the logo.

**Pattern 3: Free Survey / Free Consultation as Primary CTA**
"Book your Free Survey" or "Request an Appointment" is the universal primary CTA. The word "Free" removes the financial objection that would prevent a stranger from inviting someone into their home. Every competitor uses this framing. Adams Blinds uses "BOOK AN APPOINTMENT" without "Free" — a missed opportunity.

**Pattern 4: Room-Based Product Navigation**
All six competitors offer room-based browsing (Living Room, Bedroom, Kitchen, Bathroom) as a secondary navigation layer. This is how consumers naturally think — not by blind type. Shoppers know they need "something for the bedroom" before they know they want a "blackout roman blind".

**Pattern 5: Measurement / Guarantee Risk Reversal**
Both ecommerce players (247 Blinds, Blinds 2 Go) invest heavily in measurement guarantees (MeaSUREguard, SureSize). The fitted-service players (Hillarys, Thomas Sanderson, Shuttercraft) remove the measurement anxiety entirely by having professionals measure for you. Adams Blinds has a free temporary blinds offer that does this better than anyone — it's just not communicated prominently.

## PART 3: COMPETITOR WEAKNESSES — TOP 5 THINGS THEY'RE DOING BADLY THAT ADAMS BLINDS CAN BEAT

**Weakness 1: Discount Dependency (Hillarys, 247 Blinds, Blinds 2 Go, Thomas Sanderson, Shutterly Fabulous)**
Five of six competitors lead with percentage-off discounts (40%, 70%, 60%, 25%). This cheapens premium products and trains customers to wait for sales. Adams Blinds DOES NOT discount — this is a genuine competitive advantage. The brand needs to actively contrast itself as "the alternative to discount blinds."

**Weakness 2: Anonymous National Positioning**
Every major competitor is a national brand with no London identity whatsoever. Hillarys says "your local advisor" but doesn't know London. Thomas Sanderson is from Nottingham. None of them can speak to London homes, London architecture (Victorian bay windows, Edwardian sash windows, Georgian townhouses), London lifestyles, or London postcodes. Adams Blinds is a London company. This is a complete positioning whitespace.

**Weakness 3: Weak Photography — Stock/Staged vs Real London Homes**
Competitors use generic lifestyle photography that could be set anywhere in the UK. Adams Blinds, as a London fitting company, has access to real London homes and real before/after transformations in recognisable London settings. This creates authenticity that national brands cannot replicate.

**Weakness 4: Cold, Corporate Visual Language**
Dark grey, deep purple, functional typography. No warmth. None of the competitors have a visual identity that feels genuinely premium in the way luxury home brands do. The cream/terracotta/aged brass palette of Adams Blinds, if properly implemented, would be visually distinctive across the entire category.

**Weakness 5: Poor Trust Signal Hierarchy**
Competitors scatter trust signals throughout pages rather than concentrating them at the key decision moment. Most bury their review counts, place awards only in footers, and don't show real review text near CTAs. Adams Blinds should place its 4.9★ / 129 reviews immediately adjacent to every primary CTA.

## PART 4: PREMIUM DESIGN LESSONS — WHAT AESOP / WHITE COMPANY / SOHO HOUSE DO

**Aesop — The Editorial Voice**
- Every product description uses precise, evocative language: "Herbaceous, woody, spicy." "For renewed, replenished, resilient skin."
- Products are tagged with editorial labels: "Beloved formulation" / "New addition" — not "Best seller" or "Sale"
- The site uses a quotation from Ada Lovelace as a section divider — nothing to do with skincare, everything to do with brand character
- "Recommended reading" section treats customers as intellectually engaged — not just buyers
- Amber glass bottle imagery on plain backgrounds — the packaging IS the photography
- Certified B Corp and Leaping Bunny badges used as ethical trust signals, not commercial ones
- Lesson for Adams Blinds: Lead with craftsmanship language. "Precision-fitted." "Measured to the millimetre." Use product descriptors that speak to artistry.

**The White Company — Warmth Through Restraint**
- Sophisticated DynamicYield recommendation engine — personalization at luxury scale
- Multi-currency, multi-region presence signals global luxury credibility
- Amplience CDN for responsive imagery — performance as quality signal
- Navigation hover delay of 750ms — slowing the interaction slightly creates a sense of deliberateness
- Mobile-first design decisions are not retrofitted — genuinely responsive
- Lesson for Adams Blinds: Every interaction should feel intentional. Hover states, transitions, and micro-animations should feel crafted, not automatic.

**Soho House — Understated Authority**
- Typography: HK Grotesk (clean sans) paired with Cardo (editorial serif) — exactly the kind of pairing Adams Blinds should use with Candara + Bodoni
- Colour system: curated neutral-to-jewel tones. Off-black (#121212), cream (#F0EEEB), muted greens, warm oranges — no bright primaries
- 8px modular spacing system — all whitespace is deliberate, never accidental
- Max-width cap at 2120px — refuses to sprawl on large screens. Content stays dignified.
- Hover states use opacity shifts, not colour changes — subtlety signals confidence
- Location becomes narrative: "Greek Street", "Dean Street", "Shoreditch" — places become experiences
- Member-only features get subtle positioning, not aggressive pop-ups
- Lesson for Adams Blinds: London neighbourhoods ARE the brand story. "Fitted in Hampstead. Trusted in Notting Hill. Recommended in Islington." Geographic specificity creates authority that national brands cannot copy.

**Combined Premium Design Principles:**
1. Typography hierarchy carries brand personality — not just readability
2. Negative space is an active design element, not unused real estate
3. Editorial labels elevate products beyond commodity
4. Location/geography becomes story, not just logistics
5. Ethical credentials are displayed as values, not badges
6. Interactions are calibrated to feel deliberate and premium
7. The writing voice is consistent across every touchpoint

## PART 5: CONVERSION PATTERNS — CTA APPROACHES, FORM PLACEMENT, URGENCY

**What Works in This Category:**

**Primary CTA: "Book Your FREE Survey" outperforms "Get a Quote"**
Every high-converting fitted-blinds site uses the "Free" modifier on the survey CTA. It removes the first objection (cost of committing). "GET A QUOTE" (Adams Blinds current) implies a quote might cost something. "Book your FREE Home Survey" is the proven template.

**WhatsApp as CTA (Adams Blinds advantage)**
Adams Blinds already has a WhatsApp floating button — this is genuinely ahead of competitors. None of the national brands offer WhatsApp contact. It should be elevated to a primary CTA alongside the phone number, not hidden as a floating element.

**Form Placement: Above the Fold on a Dedicated Section**
The highest-converting pattern is: Hero image → trust bar → single-field lead form (postcode or name/phone) immediately below. Shuttercraft's postcode field is the clearest example. The user gives one piece of information to start the journey — low friction.

**Urgency Triggers Used by Competitors:**
- "Express Shutters: fitted in as little as two weeks" (Shutterly Fabulous) — speed urgency
- "Up to 70% off" (247 Blinds) — discount urgency (Adams Blinds should NOT use this)
- "Finance Options Available" (Hillarys) — affordability reassurance
- "Fitted within 5 weeks" (Hillarys UK-made shutters) — delivery certainty

**Urgency Triggers for Adams Blinds (Non-Discount):**
- "Free survey this week — [X] slots remaining in [postcode area]" — scarcity without discount
- "Currently fitting in [neighbourhood] — book to coincide" — social proof + logistics
- "Most London blinds need 3-4 weeks lead time — book your survey now" — calendar urgency

**Form Best Practices:**
- Single entry point (name + phone, or postcode) above fold
- Multi-step progressive form reduces perceived friction
- "No obligation" language near submit button
- Trust signal (star rating + count) immediately adjacent to submit

## PART 6: TRUST SIGNAL PLACEMENT — WHERE AND HOW TRUST SIGNALS CONVERT BEST

**Finding from competitor analysis:**

The most effective placement pattern (used by Hillarys, Shuttercraft):
1. **Top bar (persistent):** 3-4 trust icons — guarantee, rating, years in business, free element
2. **Immediately below hero:** Star rating + review count as text + link ("4.9★ on Google — 129 reviews")
3. **Adjacent to primary CTA:** One-line trust statement ("Award-winning service. BBSA member.")
4. **Mid-page review block:** 3-4 named testimonials with specificity (name, location, product)
5. **Above footer:** Full trust signal consolidation — awards, accreditations, guarantee icons

**What Adams Blinds should do differently:**
- Current: Awards and accreditations buried in footer — almost invisible
- Target: Awards displayed as editorial hero elements, not compliance badges
- BUILD 2021 award deserves image treatment, not a text link
- 4.9★ / 129 Google reviews must appear within the first scroll on every page
- BBSA badge should appear near CTA, not in footer
- Insurance certificates and ICO registration belong in a "credentials" section, not scattered through footer links

**Review Display Best Practice:**
- Show star rating numerically (4.9) AND as stars AND as count (129 reviews)
- Link directly to Google review page — verified third-party credibility
- Testimonials: include first name + London area code (e.g., "James T., Islington") + date
- Include at least one commercial client testimonial on homepage — signals professional capability

## PART 7: PSYCHOLOGICAL TRIGGERS MISSING — CIALDINI PRINCIPLES COMPETITORS UNDERUSE

**1. Social Proof — Specificity Gap**
All competitors show star ratings but almost none quantify neighbourhood penetration. "Fitted in over 800 London homes" or "Trusted by homeowners in SW1, N1, E2, W11" is far more convincing than a national aggregate rating. Hyper-local social proof is unused by every competitor.

**2. Authority — Process Transparency**
Thomas Sanderson comes closest with its 4-step process nav, but no competitor fully documents WHY their fitting process produces better results. Adams Blinds should show the technical expertise: how they measure bay windows differently from sash windows, why professional fitting matters for longevity, what happens when a blind is measured incorrectly (and how they prevent it).

**3. Scarcity — Availability Communication**
No competitor tells you how busy they are or how far out bookings go. "Currently booking 3 weeks ahead in Zone 2" is honest, creates appropriate urgency, and signals demand. This is entirely unused.

**4. Liking — Founder/Team Humanisation**
Every competitor is a faceless company. Nobody shows the fitter who'll come to your home, the person who answers the phone, the founder's story. For a service where a stranger comes into your home, this is a massive anxiety. Adams Blinds can show faces, introduce the team, and make the fitter known before they arrive.

**5. Reciprocity — The Free Survey Underexploited**
Competitors offer free surveys as a tactical lead gen mechanism. The premium framing would be "we give you an expert consultation worth £150 at no charge" — emphasising the gift's value rather than its zero cost. Same offer, radically different perceived value.

**6. Commitment and Consistency — Micro-Commitments**
No competitor uses progressive commitment well. A free fabric sample order (small commitment) followed by a survey request (medium commitment) followed by an appointment (full commitment) is the proven pattern. Adams Blinds has fabric samples — but the pathway from sample to survey needs to be explicit and automatic.

**7. Unity — Shared London Identity**
Cialdini's newest principle: shared identity. "We're a London company. We know London homes." This isn't just positioning — it's belonging. No national competitor can credibly claim to be "one of us." Adams Blinds can own this entirely.
